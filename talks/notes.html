Introduction to tilings

We are all familiar to some degree with the way tiles are used to provide functional and aesthetic coverings to floors and walls in human constructions. The way that one or more basic tile shapes can be used in a reetitive way to cover a surface motivates the idea of a mathematical tiling.

Basic definition

The term tiling refers to a covering of the plane \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with a collection of shapes called tiles. We normally assume that the tiles are all toplogical discs, which means roughly that they are each a continuous deformation of a disc, that their boundary is a simple closed curve and that they do not contain any holes. In the tiling we assume that the whole plane has been covered and that the tiles do not overlap, except where two tiles meet and share part of their boundaries. For examples of mathematical tilings consult the many figures in Tilings by Regular polygons -- II by Chavey (1989) and The eighty-one types of isohedral tilings in the plane by Grunbaum & Shephard (1977). A mathematical problem that tilings present is, in a nutshell, to classify them, to be able to say when two given tilings, although they may seem superficially different, are nevertheless of the same type.

Note that the tilings in Chavey's paper all consist of tiles which are regular polygons, though a tiling may feature tiles of more than one shape, whereas in Grunbaum & Shephard's paper some of the tilings shown feature tiles that are not regular poygons, though in each tiling, all the tiles are the same shape. A moment's reflection should reveal that to attempt a classification of all tilings as we have defined them in the previouys paragraph would be a monumental task due to the bewildering number of possibilities. So all the classifications that have been carried out to date always begin by placing careful restrictions on the tilings considered and precisely defining a notion of the type of a tiling, and then proceeding to carry out the classification.

Symmetry

Classifications of geometric constructions such as tilings often involve describing the symmetries of the construction. A symmetry of a tiling of the plane is an isometric transformation of the plane which preserves the tiling as a whole.

Isometries of the plane

An isometric transformation, or isometry, of the plane is a bijective (one-to-one and onto) mapping \(\phi : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2\) which preserves lengths of vectors, i.e. \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad |\phi(\mathbf{x})| = | \mathbf{x} |.\]

A non-identity isometry, \(\phi\), of the plane is always one of three types: a translation, a rotation, a reflection or a glide reflection. The identity isometry, the translations and rotations are all referred to as direct isometries. The reflections and glide reflections are referred to as indirect ismetries.

Translations

A translation by a fixed vector \(\mathbf{d}\) is the isometry \(\phi\) defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{d}.\]

Rotations

A rotation about the origin by an angle \(\theta\) is the isometry \(\phi\) defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x},\] where \(A\) is the rotation matrix \[A = \begin{pmatrix}\cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{pmatrix}.\]

In general, a rotation about a fixed point, with position vector \(\mathbf{c}\), through an angle \(\theta\) is the isometry \(\phi\) defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{c} + A(\mathbf{x}- \mathbf{c}),\] where \(A\) is the rotation matrix above.

Reflections

A reflection in the line through the origin making an angle of \(\theta\) with the \(x\)-axis is the isometry defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x},\] where \(A\) is the reflection matrix \[A = \begin{pmatrix}\cos 2\theta & \sin 2\theta \\ \sin 2\theta & -\cos 2\theta \end{pmatrix}.\]

In general, a relection in the line through a point \(\mathbf{c}\) which makes an angle of \(\theta\) with the line through \(\mathbf{c}\) parallel to the \(x\)-axis the isometry defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{c} + A(\mathbf{x}- \mathbf{c}),\] where \(A\) is the reflection matrix above.

Glide reflections

A glide reflection consists of a reflection followed by a translation by a vector \(\mathbf{g}\), where \(\mathbf{g}\) is parallel to the direction of the reflection axis. A glide reflection can be expressed as the isometry \(\phi\) defined by \[ \forall \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2 \quad \phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{g} + \psi(\mathbf{x}),\] where \(\psi\) is a reflection as defined in the previous section.

Symmetry group of \(X\)

If you have two symmetries, \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\) of an object \(X\), i.e. isometries that leave the object unchanged as a whole, then if we compose the isometries as \(\phi_3 = \phi_2 \circ \phi_1\), then \(\phi_3\) will again be an isometry and also a symmetry of \(X\).

A symmetry group of a geometric object \(X\) in the plane is the group consisting of all isometries of the plane which are symmetries of \(X\), i.e. leave \(X\) unchanged as a whole. The group operation in a symmetry group is always that of composition. In your work on tilings you will encounter the dihedral groups \(D_n\), \(n \geq 3\). The dihedral group \(D_n\) is the group of symmetries of a regular \(n\)-sided polygon. The elements of the group \(D_n\) can be expressed as \[D_n = \left \{ r^i , r^i s \, : \, 1 \leq i \leq n-1 \right \},\] where \(r\) is the rotation about the centre of the polygon by an angle of \(2\pi/n\) in a counter-clockwise direction and \(s\) is any reflectional symmetry in one of the axes of reflectional symmetry of the polygon. Note that \(r^0 =e\), the identity symmetry, and \(r\) and \(s\) satisfy the relation \[sr^i = r^{-i} s, \quad (1 \leq i \leq n-1).\] Here the products \(xy\) always denote the composition \(x \circ y\) and in these dihedral groups \(r^{-i} = r^{m-i}\) for \(1 \leq i \leq m-1\).

Glossary of terms

Here is a list of some of the common technical terms you will encounter in reading the Grunbaum & Shephard paper but that aren't defined there, along with a brief definition or reference where you can find it.

  • closed topological disc: as defined above, a region of the plane which is a continuous deformation of a disc, whose boundary is a simple closed curve and the region does not contain any holes. The prefix closed indicates that the region includes the points that make up its boundary.
  • congruent: two regions of the plane are congruent if they are related by an isometry and maybe a scaling transformation, i.e. if they are the 'same' shape.
  • connected set: a subset of the plane is connected if it is made up of one single piece. Defining this precisely and rigourously is quite technical, but for our purposes of tilings it simply means that a tile consists of a single 'piece of material' and does not consist of the union of two or more disjoint pieces.
  • cyclic group \(C_n\): the cyclic group \(C_n\) is the group of \(n\) elements that can be generated by a single element.
  • dihedral group \(D_n\): see section above.
  • disjoint: two sets are disjoint if their intersection is empty.
  • isomorphic: two groups are isomorphic if they have the same group structure, i.e. if there exists a bijective mapping, \(\phi\), between them which satisfies the homorphism property \(\phi(xy) = \phi(x)\phi(y)\).
  • subgroup: a subgroup \(H\) of a group \(G\) is a subset \(H \subset G\) such that \(H\) is itself a group under the same group operation working in \(G\).
