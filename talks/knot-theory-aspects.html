Equivalence of knots

  • A (mathematical) knot is an embedding of a simple closed loop into three-dimensional space.

  • Two knots are equivalent if one can be transformed into the other without cutting the loop.
  • A fundamental problem then is to have a way to decide whether two given knots are equivalent, or not.

Knot invariants

  • There is no known single algorithm that can decide whether any two knots are equivalent.
  • So knot classification seems to be a very hard problem. Which is good.
  • Knot theorists develop mathematical tools, called invariants, that are partially succesful at this.
  • Given a knot diagram, an invariant produces a number, polynomial or some other mathematical object. If these values are different, then the pair of knot diagrams are not equivalent. But if the values are the same, then no conclusion is reached.
  • Any particular invariant can detect inequivalence for some pairs of knot diagrams, but not for others.

The table of knots

  • Generate all possible knot diagrams with a fixed number of crossings.
  • Use various procedures for eliminating duplicates, diagrams that are equivalent to knots with fewer crossings, knots which decompose as a sum of two or more simpler knots.
  • Left with a set of diagrams which are candidates for all the knot types of that crossing number.
  • Use all the invariants at your disposal and other ad hoc methods to confirm that the resulting diagrams represent inequivalent knots.
  • So far the prime knots to 14 crossings have been tabulated.

The Alexander invariants

  • Every knot is the boundary of a two-dimensional Seifert surface.
  • The space around the knot (complement of the knot) is cut open along the Seifert surface, and an infinite number of copies of this cut-open complement are glued together along the exposed Seifert surfaces to produce the infinite cyclic cover of the knot complement.
  • This covering space is analyzed by considering the space of all possible loops we can embed in it.
  • This space of loops leads to an algebraic mathematical object called the Alexander module.
  • My PhD work concerned algorithms that solved the decision problem for associated objects, called the Alexander ideals.

The fundamental group

  • Many mathematical spaces (including knot complements) are studied by consider the collection of all the possible ways of embedding a loop into the space.
  • This collection of loops leads to an object called the fundamental group.
